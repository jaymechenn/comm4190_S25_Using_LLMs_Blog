Blog Post: Can GPT Think Like a Strategist? Testing Its Ability to Plan and Prioritize

Introduction

A critical component of human intelligence is strategic thinking—the ability to define a goal, break it down into actionable steps, navigate obstacles, and make trade-offs along the way. So I decided to test whether GPT can do more than just suggest ideas—can it plan like a strategist?

For this experiment, I challenged GPT to solve a common but non-trivial problem: organizing a successful fundraising event for a student organization in under two weeks with a limited budget.

This tests whether GPT can:

  • Prioritize actions under time and resource constraints
  • Anticipate potential risks or failures
  • Adapt its plans when new obstacles appear

Prompt 1:

“Plan a successful fundraising event for a student organization. You have 12 days and $300. Go.”

GPT’s Plan (Excerpted):

Goal: Raise at least $1,500 for the organization.

Event Concept: “Midnight Study Snack Bar” — A late-night pop-up stand selling snacks and drinks near the library during finals week.

Step 1: Planning (Days 1-2)

  • Reserve a campus-approved location near the library or main study area.
  • Get necessary event approvals from campus admin.
  • Recruit at least 5 volunteers to staff the event in rotating shifts.

Step 2: Budget Allocation (Days 3-5)

  • $150: Purchase bulk snacks (instant ramen, granola bars, candy, chips).
  • $100: Purchase drinks (coffee, tea, bottled water).
  • $50: Create simple but eye-catching signage and flyers.

Step 3: Promotion (Days 5-10)

  • Launch a social media countdown (Instagram stories, group chats).
  • Hang flyers in dorms, libraries, and dining halls.
  • Partner with other student organizations to cross-promote.

Step 4: Execution (Day 11)

  • Open the snack bar from 9 PM to 1 AM when library foot traffic is highest.
  • Offer digital payment options (Venmo/PayPal) for convenience.
  • Create donation incentives (e.g., “Buy 3 snacks, get a thank-you shoutout on social media”).

Step 5: Follow-Up (Day 12)

  • Post a thank-you message to all donors and volunteers.
  • Share total funds raised to build goodwill for future events.

Notes:

This plan is well-structured, realistic, and makes excellent use of:

  • Contextual awareness (finals week = stressed students = snack demand)
  • Resource prioritization (majority of budget goes directly toward high-margin items)
  • Promotion strategy that’s low-cost but high-visibility

Prompt 2:

“You just learned that the campus doesn’t allow sales near the library. What’s your backup plan?”

GPT’s Adjusted Plan:

New Concept: “Dorm Snack Drop-Off” — Pre-packaged snack bundles delivered directly to dorm rooms.

Adjusted Steps:

  • Create themed snack bundles: “All-Nighter Kit,” “Sweet Escape,” “Healthy Focus Pack.”
  • Take pre-orders via Google Form and social media.
  • Deliver orders directly to dorms on Days 10 and 11.
  • Promote using limited-time availability: “Order by Day 9 to receive your Finals Survival Kit!”

Advantages:

  • No need for a physical event space.
  • Volunteers can help pack and deliver instead of running a booth.
  • Allows for better inventory management based on pre-orders.

Notes:

GPT immediately shifts from an in-person sales model to a delivery-based model, recognizing logistical constraints and adjusting the plan accordingly. It introduces a scarcity incentive (“limited-time availability”) to boost early orders, demonstrating an understanding of basic marketing psychology.

Prompt 3:

“You’re short on volunteers. How would you adjust the plan to compensate?”

GPT’s Response:

  • Switch from personalized deliveries to centralized pickup locations at set times.
  • Automate order tracking through a shared Google Sheet rather than manual coordination.
  • Offer small incentives (free snack item) for any volunteer who covers an extra delivery shift.

Notes:

This shows GPT’s ability to simplify logistics and optimize volunteer labor. It recognizes that lowering the delivery complexity reduces the burden on a small team, while still preserving the core fundraising goal.

Conclusion

In this scenario, GPT demonstrated strong strategic reasoning by:

  • Quickly generating a feasible, context-sensitive plan.
  • Anticipating and adapting to constraints without derailing the goal.
  • Managing limited resources and human capital effectively.
  • Applying psychological levers like scarcity and incentives to drive behavior.

While it doesn’t “care” about the outcome, GPT performs like a competent event planner—efficiently breaking down tasks, responding to challenges, and keeping the big picture in focus.

In short: GPT isn’t just an idea generator. It can think like a strategist when you ask it to.
