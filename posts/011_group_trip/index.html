Introduction

We’ve all been there: trying to plan a trip with a group of friends, each with their own preferences, dietary needs, and chaotic sleep schedules. So I decided to throw that exact challenge at GPT.

The goal? To see whether an LLM can simulate realistic, preference-sensitive planning—not just pick destinations, but balance personalities, anticipate social dynamics, and make the itinerary actually work. This hits at several course themes:
- Can LLMs plan across time and tasks with human-like coherence?
- Do they simulate others’ beliefs, needs, and reactions?
- Can they juggle constraints and tradeoffs, not just generate pretty lists?

Prompt 1:

“Plan a weekend getaway for four college friends. One is vegetarian, one is outdoorsy, one loves nightlife, and one is anxious about logistics.”

GPT’s Response (excerpted):

Location: Asheville, North Carolina

Friday Night:
- Arrival and check-in to a cozy Airbnb cabin in the mountains (less stressful than a hotel for the anxious planner).
- Casual dinner at a local vegetarian-friendly spot (Laughing Seed Café).
- Light games and wine at the cabin—low-pressure social time.

Saturday:
- Morning hike on the Blue Ridge Parkway (outdoorsy win)
- Afternoon downtown exploration (cafés + bookstores = neutral ground)
- Dinner at a place with both cocktails and mocktails (for nightlife-lover and anxious friend)
- Evening option: rooftop bar or stay-in movie night—group vote

Sunday:
- Brunch (with vegetarian options)
- Optional nature walk vs. spa split (let people choose their vibe)
- Group debrief over ice cream before heading back

Notes & Analysis

This was way better than I expected. GPT isn’t just listing activities—it’s demonstrating constraint-aware planning and empathic logic. Here’s what it got right:

1. Identity Balancing

Each friend’s personality is acknowledged—not in isolation, but in how they’d influence the flow of the weekend: - The anxious planner gets early check-in and a low-key first night - The vegetarian gets multiple meal options - The nightlife lover gets one wild card night but not an entire agenda - The outdoorsy one gets a hike, but not the whole weekend outdoors

This shows simulated theory of mind across multiple agents—not just reasoning about one user, but many at once.

2. Soft Power Planning

GPT uses optional branching (e.g., vote-based evening, spa or walk) to defuse social tension. This is a highly pragmatic move—a way of modeling group dynamics and pre-emptively resolving conflict. It feels like something a seasoned group-text veteran would do.

3. Temporal Coherence

Activities flow in a natural rhythm—arrival → food → unwind → activity → downtime → closing reflection. GPT doesn’t just plan what, but when—an important sign of event planning fluency and commonsense time modeling.

Prompt 2:

“What if the vegetarian friend is also gluten-free and hates hiking?”

GPT’s Adjusted Plan (excerpted):

  • Swap the hike for a morning scenic drive + farmer’s market visit (still scenic, but less physically intense)
  • Ensure all meal spots offer gluten-free options (e.g., Posana Restaurant)
  • Add a crafting or pottery class Saturday afternoon—creative and inclusive
  • Keep Sunday’s spa option as a featured activity for this friend

Notes:

GPT instantly re-weights the itinerary based on updated constraints. It avoids just cutting activities—instead, it replaces them with comparable alternatives (e.g., nature viewing instead of hiking). This shows it understands functional equivalence—how to maintain the spirit of inclusion without sacrificing group diversity.

Prompt 3:

“Can you write the group text where someone proposes this plan?”

GPT’s Group Text:

Hey y’all! I sketched out a super chill Asheville plan based on everyone’s vibe: vegetarian/gf-friendly eats, outdoorsy options (but no intense hikes), chill cabin nights + one optional bar hop. Also added some stuff like pottery/spa if we want to split up Saturday. LMK thoughts! Happy to tweak! 🤍🌲✨

Notes:

Chef’s kiss. GPT nails groupchat tone:
- Casual but inclusive
- Emoji-laced but not overdone
- Balances initiative-taking with flexibility (“Happy to tweak!”)

This shows GPT can simulate socio-linguistic variation—not just the plan, but the social performance of proposing a plan.

Conclusion

This experiment shows GPT is surprisingly good at: - Modeling preferences and personalities - Sequencing tasks over time with emotional intelligence - Writing in socially appropriate ways to different audiences

While it’s not really “understanding” the tension of planning a trip with humans, it generates a remarkably realistic approximation of someone who does—with empathy, structure, and flexibility.

GPT might not need the weekend off, but it sure knows how to plan one.
