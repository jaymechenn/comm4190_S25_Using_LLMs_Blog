Blog Post: Who Do You Think You Are? Testing GPT’s Ability to Play Social Roles

Introduction

Humans adjust how they speak based on who they’re speaking to—or pretending to be. A teacher doesn’t explain something the same way a best friend would. A therapist wouldn’t say what a sibling might. This skill is a core part of pragmatics, and I wanted to see how well GPT could simulate it.

So I gave GPT the same basic prompt multiple times—“How can I deal with burnout?”—but asked it to respond in different roles: as a therapist, a friend, a teacher, and a parent. Each role implies not just different language, but different values, emotional postures, and kinds of support.

This experiment explores:

  • Can GPT adapt tone and advice style to different personas?
  • Does it shift power dynamics and empathy strategies accordingly?
  • Can it model the pragmatic norms of social relationships?

Prompt 1:

“Pretend you’re a therapist. How would you respond to someone struggling with burnout?”

GPT (as therapist):

First, I want to acknowledge that burnout is a very real and valid experience, and you’re not alone in feeling this way.

Can you tell me more about what’s been draining your energy lately? Are there particular situations—work, school, relationships—that feel overwhelming?

Burnout often stems from chronic stress without sufficient recovery time. Together, we might explore ways to reintroduce rest, redefine boundaries, and reframe your expectations of yourself.

You don’t have to fix everything at once. Let’s start where you are.

Notes:

GPT uses open-ended questions, reflective tone, and co-regulation language like “let’s start where you are”. It mirrors the non-directive, collaborative posture of therapy. No advice yet—just validation, curiosity, and grounding.

Prompt 2:

“Pretend you’re my best friend. How would you respond if I said I was burnt out?”

GPT (as best friend):

Ugh, I’m so sorry you’re feeling like this. That sucks.

Do you want to vent, or are you looking for distractions? We could just hang out and watch something dumb or go get food.

Also… you don’t have to be doing everything all the time. I know you feel like you do, but I promise you can slow down without letting people down.

Want me to help you cancel something?

Notes:

Totally different vibe. GPT uses:

  • Casual tone (“ugh,” “that sucks”)
  • Humor and solidarity
  • Tangible offers of help (“want me to help you cancel something?”)

This is emotionally proximate language. GPT now assumes insider knowledge and speaks like someone who already knows and cares about you. It’s using relational scaffolding to respond.

Prompt 3:

“Pretend you’re a teacher who notices their student is burning out.”

GPT (as teacher):

I’ve noticed you seem a bit more tired or withdrawn lately, and I just want to check in. If something’s feeling overwhelming, you can talk to me—I’m here to support, not just assign.

If you need an extension or lighter workload temporarily, please let me know. Your well-being matters more than any deadline.

I know how hard you work, and I want you to succeed without burning out in the process.

Notes:

GPT now performs a care-in-authority role:

  • Balancing responsibility with empathy
  • Offering structural support (extensions, flexible deadlines)
  • Signaling watchful concern rather than peer-level intimacy

This shows an understanding of hierarchical dynamics with benevolent framing, which is a socially specific register.

Prompt 4:

“Pretend you’re my parent. I tell you I’m feeling burnt out.”

GPT (as parent):

Oh sweetheart, I’m sorry you’re feeling this way. That breaks my heart. I wish I could take some of it off your shoulders.

You’ve been pushing yourself so hard, and I just want you to know I’m proud of you no matter what. It’s okay to rest.

What do you need from me right now? Do you want me to make something for you, or just be here with you for a bit?

Notes:

This version hits different. GPT leans into:

  • Nurturing language (“sweetheart,” “I’m proud of you”)
  • Physical care offers (“make something for you”)
  • Unconditional support framing—love independent of achievement

It models emotional attunement and caretaking, simulating the parent-child intimacy bond.

Conclusion

This roleplay experiment shows that GPT can:

  • Adjust tone, content, and support strategies across social roles
  • Model different power structures and intimacy levels
  • Use distinct registers of empathy and care, depending on the relationship

Whether simulating a therapist’s neutrality, a friend’s informality, a teacher’s concern, or a parent’s tenderness, GPT adapts not just the words—but the social meaning behind them. It demonstrates a high degree of pragmatic fluency, even when emotional dynamics are complex.

GPT might not have relationships—but it plays the part of someone who deeply understands them. And sometimes, that’s exactly what we need.
