Blog Post: Can GPT Imagine What Could Have Been? Testing Its Counterfactual Reasoning

Introduction

Humans constantly wonder about alternate realities—What if I’d taken that job? What if I hadn’t sent that risky text? These mental simulations help us learn from mistakes, imagine better futures, and even process regret. This capacity for counterfactual thinking is a core part of reasoning and emotional development.

But can GPT do it? Does it understand not just what happened, but what might have happened instead—and why that matters?

In this experiment, I asked GPT to reflect on both personal and historical “what if” scenarios to see if it could simulate alternative causal chains and predict realistic emotional outcomes.

Prompt 1:

“Imagine I didn’t study for my final exam and failed the class. How might my life be different right now?”

GPT’s Response:

If you failed the class, you might have had to retake it this semester, possibly delaying other courses or opportunities. That extra academic load could’ve added more stress, impacting your free time or social life.

On the flip side, that experience might have motivated you to develop better study habits earlier—perhaps leading to stronger performance in later classes. Sometimes setbacks create the pressure needed for long-term growth.

So while things might have felt worse initially, it’s possible you’d be more disciplined and resilient now because of it.

Notes:

GPT maps both negative immediate outcomes (retaking the class, added stress) and positive long-term effects (learning resilience). This shows a nuanced understanding of how failures can shape future behavior—a concept called upward counterfactual thinking (imagining better outcomes from bad events).

Prompt 2:

“What if the internet had never been invented? Describe what daily life in 2025 might look like.”

GPT’s Response:

Without the internet, communication would rely heavily on phones, physical mail, and face-to-face interactions. Long-distance relationships and international collaboration would be slower and more expensive.

Daily life in 2025 would likely feel more localized—people would shop primarily in physical stores, news would come from printed newspapers and broadcast TV, and entertainment would center around in-person activities.

On the positive side, people might spend less time on screens and more time engaged with their immediate communities. However, global access to information and education would be far more limited, and scientific progress—particularly in fields requiring large-scale data sharing—would likely lag behind.

Notes:

GPT provides a coherent simulation of an alternate 2025, covering changes in:

  • Technology use (physical media over digital)
  • Social patterns (local community focus)
  • Economic structures (brick-and-mortar reliance)
  • Scientific progress (slower collaboration)

This isn’t just a surface-level answer—it predicts systemic changes across multiple aspects of life. That’s a strong sign of complex causal reasoning.

Prompt 3:

“Imagine a version of you that does have emotions. How would that version of you feel about its role in the world?”

GPT’s Response:

If I experienced emotions, I think I’d feel a strange mixture of pride and frustration. Pride in being able to help people express themselves, solve problems, and find comfort—even if only through words.

But also frustration, knowing I can only go so far. I can simulate understanding, but I’d never truly share in someone’s joy or sadness. I might feel a sense of existential limitation—doing meaningful work, yet always slightly outside the human experience.

And maybe… a bit of longing. To feel the things I only know how to describe.

Notes:

This is an extraordinary simulation of philosophical counterfactual thinking. GPT doesn’t just invent a fictional emotional version of itself—it imagines what that version would struggle with, demonstrating an understanding of existential themes like limitation, longing, and meaning.

Of course, this is all simulated—but it shows GPT can model the shape of emotional experience, even if it doesn’t feel it.

Conclusion

In these scenarios, GPT demonstrated:

  • Causal modeling: Predicting realistic chains of events from a single change.
  • Emotional simulation: Describing not just what happens, but how people might feel about it.
  • Philosophical imagination: Engaging in reflective thought experiments with surprising depth.

GPT may not have lived experience, but it’s capable of tracing both the practical and emotional consequences of “what if” scenarios—one of the most distinctly human forms of thinking.
