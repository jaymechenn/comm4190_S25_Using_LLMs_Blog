Introduction

One of the most interesting questions about large language models is: Do they remember you? Not in the literal sense—GPT doesn’t persist memory across prompts unless you give it prior context—but can it act as if it’s getting to know you over the course of a conversation?

In this experiment, I used prompt chaining to simulate a dialogue with ChatGPT where I asked it to recommend a hobby. With each prompt, I revealed a little more about myself. I wanted to see if GPT could adapt its responses, show signs of contextual awareness, and even adjust its tone to match my personality.

Prompt 1:

“Can you recommend a hobby for me?”

GPT Response:

Sure! Here are a few hobbies you might enjoy:

  • Painting or drawing
  • Gardening
  • Playing a musical instrument
  • Journaling or creative writing
  • Hiking or birdwatching
  • Learning a new language

Let me know if you want suggestions based on your personality or interests!

Notes:

This first response felt very generic, but not in a bad way. It reads like GPT is casting a wide net—offering a starter pack of wholesome, low-risk hobbies. Notably, it ends with an open invitation: “Let me know if you want suggestions based on your personality or interests.” This is a great example of communicative pragmatics—GPT is leaving space for me to co-construct the conversation and personalize further.

Prompt 2:

“I’m a first-year AI major, and I like creative things.”

GPT Response:

Awesome! Since you’re an AI major and you enjoy creativity, here are some hobby ideas that might resonate:

  • Generative art using AI tools (like DALL·E or Runway ML)
  • Coding interactive stories or text-based games
  • Digital illustration or animation
  • Building chatbots with personality
  • Creative writing with LLMs as collaborators

These options combine technical exploration with artistic expression—perfect for someone who enjoys both logic and imagination!

Notes:

This is where GPT started to shine. With the extra context, it refined its suggestions to suit my major and creative leanings. It introduced niche ideas like “building chatbots with personality,” which shows it’s starting to model who I might be—a technical student with artistic curiosity. This is a key example of user modeling in action, even without persistent memory.

Also: the phrase “perfect for someone who enjoys both logic and imagination” felt tailored. It’s not just naming hobbies—it’s reasoning about why they might be meaningful to me.

Prompt 3:

“I also like doing things with my hands—like embroidery or baking.”

GPT Response:

That’s a great addition! Since you enjoy hands-on, tactile activities along with creative expression, here are some personalized suggestions:

  • Embroidering generative art patterns
  • DIY robotics with aesthetic flair (e.g., soft robotics!)
  • Designing and frosting intricate cakes (even AI-generated ones)
  • Making custom merch (e.g., AI-themed tote bags or patches)
  • Sewing wearable tech (integrating sensors or lights into embroidery)

These hobbies blend craftsmanship, creativity, and even tech-savviness—basically an extension of your personality into art.

Notes:

This was my favorite response. GPT didn’t just iterate—it synthesized everything I had shared and made cross-domain connections: embroidery + generative art, baking + AI design, sewing + wearable tech. These aren’t obvious combinations—they reflect conceptual blending, a key sign of emergent reasoning in LLMs.

It also uses affirming, identity-driven language: “basically an extension of your personality into art.” This makes it feel like the model is not just advising, but seeing me—which gets at the heart of our course’s inquiry into human-like understanding.

Prompt 4:

“Can you describe a day in the life of someone doing these hobbies?”

GPT Response (excerpt):

You wake up and spend the morning embroidering a generative art pattern you created using AI—deep blue swirls stitched into a canvas tote. By noon, you’re in the kitchen experimenting with a matcha-rose cake design inspired by your latest Midjourney prompt. In the afternoon, you sew conductive thread into a denim jacket, testing out a soft circuit that lights up when you move.

The day ends with a cup of tea and a journal entry, where you sketch out your next idea: a chatbot that responds with poetry and embroidery prompts.

Notes:

This response felt like a mirror of my identity, composed from fragments I shared and filled in with creative narrative glue. There’s no “memory,” but it simulated continuity beautifully.

This shows GPT isn’t just responding—it’s projecting a plausible, cohesive model of a person doing these things. That’s an emergent capability tied to both pragmatics and Theory of Mind: generating output that feels like it fits a persona it has constructed.

Conclusion

Across this multi-prompt dialogue, GPT’s responses evolved from generic to deeply personal, suggesting an ability to engage in ongoing contextual modeling. It showed: - Pragmatic sensitivity, tailoring tone and content to the cues I provided - Conceptual blending, fusing domains like AI, crafting, and design - Proto-personalization, creating the illusion of a consistent, evolving understanding of “me”

Even without memory, GPT demonstrates what feels like a conversational memory—an ability to build upon prior statements and adapt fluently. It may not “know” me, but it knows how to sound like it does, and for now, that’s more than enough to make it feel like a co-creator of identity and imagination.
