Here’s a new post idea that focuses on analogical reasoning—a cognitive skill that’s central to problem-solving, creativity, and abstract thinking. This explores whether GPT can not only answer questions, but generate useful analogies to explain complex ideas in relatable ways.

Blog Post: Can GPT Explain the Hard Stuff? Testing Its Analogical Reasoning

Introduction

Humans often understand new or complex concepts by mapping them onto familiar experiences—this is called analogical reasoning. Teachers do it all the time: explaining electrical circuits as water flowing through pipes, or comparing memory storage to a messy desk.

But can GPT do this effectively? Does it know when an analogy will help, and can it produce good ones? In this experiment, I asked GPT to explain complex ideas using analogy and then evaluated how well those analogies actually worked.

This ties directly into our course’s focus on reasoning, conceptual modeling, and communicative pragmatics.

Prompt 1:

“Explain how a neural network learns, using an analogy a child would understand.”

GPT’s Response:

Imagine you’re trying to learn how to throw a basketball into a hoop. The first time you try, you miss. So, you adjust—maybe you throw harder or aim higher. You try again and again, adjusting each time based on whether you got closer or further from the hoop.

A neural network works the same way. It tries to guess something (like what’s in a picture), then checks if it got it right. If it didn’t, it adjusts its “aim” a little and tries again. Over time, with lots of tries, it gets better and better at making accurate guesses.

Notes:

This analogy works remarkably well. It:

  • Relates to a familiar experience (learning a skill through trial and error).
  • Captures the essence of gradient descent without using technical terms.
  • Emphasizes the importance of feedback and incremental improvement, which are core to machine learning.

This suggests GPT isn’t just capable of retrieving textbook definitions—it understands how to reframe ideas for accessibility.

Prompt 2:

“Explain the concept of overfitting using a real-world analogy.”

GPT’s Response:

Imagine you’re studying for a test, but instead of learning the general concepts, you only memorize the exact answers from last year’s exam. On test day, if the questions are exactly the same, you’ll do great. But if they’re even slightly different, you’re stuck—you don’t really understand the material, you just memorized patterns.

That’s what happens when a neural network overfits—it learns the training data perfectly, but struggles when it encounters anything new.

Notes:

This is a spot-on analogy:

  • It clearly maps memorization vs. generalization onto training vs. testing data.
  • The failure mode is familiar and intuitive to most people.
  • It helps explain why overfitting is a problem, not just what it is.

This suggests GPT understands functional similarities across domains—the heart of analogical reasoning.

Prompt 3:

“Explain why people procrastinate using a metaphor.”

GPT’s Response:

Procrastination is like carrying a backpack full of tasks. Each time you avoid one, instead of removing it, you just shuffle it around to the bottom of the bag. It feels lighter for a moment because you’re not looking at it, but eventually, you have to pull everything out—and by then, it’s even heavier with worry.

Notes:

This metaphor captures both the psychological relief of avoidance and the inevitable cost of procrastination. The idea of a “heavier” emotional burden over time is an elegant reflection of real cognitive load.

Here, GPT goes beyond explanation—it evokes an emotional truth through metaphor. That’s a highly advanced communicative skill.

Conclusion

Through this experiment, GPT demonstrated:

  • Flexible analogical reasoning—mapping complex ideas onto relatable real-world scenarios.
  • Audience awareness—adjusting metaphors to suit children or adult audiences.
  • Emotional resonance—creating metaphors that don’t just explain, but feel true.

While GPT doesn’t have its own lived experiences, it clearly knows how to simulate the way humans understand the world through analogy. And in doing so, it becomes not just a knowledge database—but a surprisingly effective teacher.

Want to try a follow-up where GPT deliberately generates bad analogies and then explains why they fail? That would be a great way to test its understanding of why analogies work—or don’t!
