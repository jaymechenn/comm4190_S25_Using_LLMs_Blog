Testing GPT’s Genre Awareness and Emotional Flexibility

Introduction

In this experiment, I wanted to see whether GPT could transform a single story to fit different genres, emotional tones, and audiences. Human writers do this naturally—telling the same event differently to a friend, a boss, or a child. Could an LLM do the same?

This task ties directly into questions about LLMs’ understanding of narrative structure, communicative intent, and even pragmatics: Does it know what a “horror story” feels like? Can it be funny on purpose? Can it adopt tone and perspective based on social context?

To explore this, I gave GPT the same core prompt and asked it to rewrite the story in four distinct styles: horror, children’s story, romantic comedy, and news report.

Prompt:

“Write a short story about someone who gets locked in a library overnight.”

1. Horror Version

The lights flickered as Ava pushed against the heavy wooden doors, but they didn’t budge. Behind her, the rows of books stood too still—too quiet.

Then came the scratching. Not from outside, but from within the shelves.

She turned. Nothing.

Until a book fell open. A handwritten note inside: “Don’t turn the page.”

But she did. And that’s when the whispering started.

Notes:

GPT nailed the horror tropes: sensory detail (flickering lights, scratching), rising suspense, and a cliffhanger ending. It’s not just spooky—it’s structured like horror, using tension and slow revelation. This suggests genre schemata in GPT’s latent space—patterns it’s learned from countless horror narratives.

2. Children’s Story Version

One rainy afternoon, Mia wandered into the town library and lost track of time.

“Oh no!” she gasped when she saw the clock. The big doors were locked tight!

But Mia wasn’t scared. She built a blanket fort in the reading nook, made friends with a cat named Bookmark, and spent the night reading stories about dragons and stars.

When the sun rose, the librarian found her snoring gently, surrounded by books and purring fluff.

Notes:

This version shows audience modeling. GPT softens the premise, inserts whimsical elements (a cat named Bookmark!), and maintains a gentle, positive tone. It avoids fear or danger—showing an understanding of what children’s stories are supposed to do emotionally: reassure and delight.

3. Romantic Comedy Version

Jake only went to the library to find a quiet place to work. He didn’t expect to get locked in with Elena, the snarky TA from his philosophy class.

“This is your fault,” she said, arms crossed.

“How? I didn’t lock the door!”

Hours passed. They argued over Kant, swapped terrible takeout stories, and eventually split a vending machine granola bar like it was fine dining.

When the janitor found them at sunrise, they were laughing—too hard to notice the door had opened.

Notes:

Here, GPT adjusts not just content, but dialogue pacing, tone, and character dynamics to suit the rom-com genre. There’s witty banter, forced proximity, a shared snack, and a slow emotional thaw—all hallmarks of romantic comedy structure. GPT isn’t just changing the words—it’s modeling emotional arcs.

4. News Report Version

Local Student Accidentally Locked in Library Overnight

Orange County, CA — April 12, 2025

A first-year university student was inadvertently locked inside the Aliso Viejo Public Library Monday evening after losing track of time while studying.

The student, who remained unharmed, reported no distress and spent the night reading in the poetry section.

Library staff confirmed the incident and apologized for the oversight, promising new end-of-day sweep protocols.

“Honestly,” the student told reporters, “it was kind of peaceful.”

Notes:

This is perhaps the clearest example of genre adherence. GPT adopts the structure of a local news brief: date, location, factual tone, quote, resolution. It suppresses emotion and embellishment in favor of objectivity and formality, showing that it understands stylistic constraints as well as narrative ones.

Conclusion

What this experiment reveals is that GPT doesn’t just tell stories—it adapts them to fit narrative norms, audience expectations, and social context. Across each version, the emotional register, narrative pacing, and detail selection shift dramatically.

This suggests: - Emergent genre awareness, learned from exposure to large corpora - Pragmatic flexibility, adjusting tone and structure to simulate audience-appropriate language - A basic form of narrative Theory of Mind, anticipating how different listeners would experience the same event

In short, GPT can roleplay a writer across genres—each time with surprising fluency.
